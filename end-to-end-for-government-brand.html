In pre-war times, when COVID still existed, Volodymyr Zelensky announced a social benefit payment program of UAH 1,000 for all fully vaccinated Ukrainians to encourage vaccination of the population and support businesses affected by quarantines.
I had 3 days to understand the essence of the program from scratch and to communicate about it with Ukraine.
During this time I created all copy for this project:

Content for the site with a step-by-step presentation of information

✅

Flow for service section in the application

Government cash assistance is not something that often happens in Ukraine, so the perception and behavior of people in such cases is turbulent.

They think: 'The government is giving money to everyone just like that,' and when they realize that not everyone will receive the money, they become angry. However, their frustration is not directed towards the government but towards Diia because the information comes from the project itself.

There were several restrictions in the eSupport program, including age, vaccination status, the requirement for special bank cards (like EBT cards), bureaucratic procedures, and limited spending options. Each of these factors contributed to dissatisfaction.
I set myself the task of anticipating every negative reaction and create a structure of information on the website to minimize these reactions as much as possible.
Anticipating in advance, I must say that I did well, but there was one significant negative reaction that I did not anticipate — a large protest by anti-vaxxers that blocked half of the government quarter in the center of Kyiv. However, in this case, it's unlikely that any form of communication could have made a difference.
[ WEBSITE ]
Knowing that a user's attention to website text resembles the letter F, I structured the information logically to present the most important details at the peak of attention.

On the homepage, I placed the essence of the program, application dates, and where to do it. I also immediately added site navigation, so people can visually see where to find answers to their questions. This navigation is most beneficial for those who return to the site and want to quickly find similar information.
On the second screen, I outlined who can receive assistance because this is the most critical filter for the audience. If a person doesn't meet at least one of the criteria, they can skip reading the rest of the site.
On the third screen, I provided a step-by-step description of what needs to be done to receive the payment, along with video explanations showing examples of the application flow for users.
On the fourth screen, I explained how to open a card for the state payments (analog of EBT card) with a list of partner banks.
Next, there was a section explaining where the money could be spent, divided into blocks.
001
First, the project's mission was presented so that people would understand that the program was created primarily to support businesses rather than discrimination of anti-vaxxers. The presence of this block was intended to reduce negativity surrounding the fact that the money couldn't be withdrawn from the card or spent on anything other than the declared categories.
002
Next, there was a large block with a simple explanation of a complex algorithm of how it all works. Here, I immediately explained the future most common problem with purchases that wouldn't go through.
003
And the visual part was designed by my designer colleague to make it easier for those who don't want to read.
Finally, there are frequently asked questions divided into categories.
[ USER FLOW ]
In the user flow section of the program, I outlined the complete path a user takes from the moment they enter the application until the money is credited to their card.
In the first 2 months of the program's existence, over 8 million Ukrainians received eSupport payments through Diia app and spent 3 billion hryvnas to support businesses. The majority of the money received through eSupport was spent on books.
